# Ashenford

## Overview

A lowland town on the only maintained road between the coastal plain and the Iron Peaks, sited at the last flat ground before the canyon passes rise into the mountains. Ashenford grew up around a Vanguard garrison that predates the current mining crisis by generations. In Kaelen's era it is a functional military town — a garrison, a supply depot, stables, a farrier, and a handful of civilian trades serving soldiers and travelers passing through toward the Peaks. In Aric's era, Ashenford has been swallowed by the Vanguard's lockdown: the garrison expanded into a fortified checkpoint across the canyon mouth, the old civilian quarter has been half-absorbed into a military compound, and the rest has collapsed into a refugee camp for displaced miners and their families who cannot pass the checkpoint to return home.

## Atmosphere & Appearance

Ashenford is built of gray ashlar stone and dark iron fittings, its buildings squat and functional rather than pretty. The main road runs straight through the town from the southern gate to the northern canyon mouth, with barracks, stables, and depot buildings flanking it. In Kaelen's era, the town feels ordered and busy — soldiers drilling in the yard, supply carts rolling north, a farrier's hammer ringing from the stable, Vanguard banners hung from iron brackets at regular intervals. The town is not welcoming, but it is professional and, if you have business, cooperative. In Aric's era, the same streets feel like a choke point. The Vanguard compound has grown, swallowing the old market and herb gardens. Refugee tents clot the southern edge of town in a makeshift camp. The banners have been replaced with newer ones in a harder gray; the old cooperative symbols are gone.

## History

Ashenford was founded a century and a half ago as a militia way-camp, when the Iron Peaks mines were still productive and the coastal towns paid cooperative tithes to maintain a force that kept the mountain passes safe from bandits and rockslides. Over generations, the militia professionalized into what became the Iron Vanguard, and the way-camp became a permanent garrison. In Kaelen's era, the Vanguard was still a regional force with cooperative obligations — its soldiers were trusted by the lowland communities, and its officers dined with village councils. After the deep-tunnel "resonance incident" that killed a patrol squad three years before Aric's quest, the Vanguard high command restructured under a new doctrine: geological containment as military priority. The Ashenford garrison was reinforced, the canyon mouth was fortified into a checkpoint, and the town's civilian life was gradually subordinated to the mission. The old tithe agreements lapsed. The lowland communities no longer speak of the Vanguard as partners.

## Notable Features

- The Garrison Square — the parade ground at the heart of Ashenford, flanked by the barracks, the armory, and the command hall. In father's era: daily drill, a notice board with militia postings. In son's era: expanded with timber walls on three sides, now effectively a military staging yard.
- The Command Hall — a two-story stone building with an iron-banded door, housing the garrison commander's office. In father's era: Captain Rhen Halloway's desk faces a map of the lowlands with Vanguard patrol routes marked in red wax. In son's era: a different commander holds the post; Rhen Halloway's old office is sealed.
- The Depot — a long warehouse of stone and timber where Vanguard supplies are staged before shipment up into the Peaks. Quartermaster Berin runs it in both eras, though his temper has soured.
- The Traveler's Hearth — the town's only civilian inn in the father's era, on the south side of town. In the son's era, it has been repurposed as a refugee aid station run by displaced villagers.
- The Refugee Camp — in son's era only, a sprawl of canvas tents, lean-tos, and carted-out wagons clustered outside the southern gate. Home to miners and their families who fled the Peaks when the tunnels were sealed and cannot get home past the checkpoint.
- The Canyon Mouth — the northern edge of town, where the road narrows into the rocky pass that leads to the Iron Peaks. In father's era: a simple gatehouse and two sentry posts. In son's era: the full Vanguard Checkpoint from the quest of that name — timber-and-stone gatehouse, flanking watchtowers, iron-reinforced gates.

## Creative Prompts

### environment-art

16-bit pixel art tileset, top-down perspective. Militarized town tiles: gray ashlar stone building walls with dark iron fittings, cobblestone-and-dirt main road, timber-frame barracks with shuttered windows, stone-and-iron armory tiles, stable tiles with split doors and straw on the floor, a farrier's forge with glowing coals visible through an open wall. Father's era palette: warm gray stone, dark-stained timber, red-and-steel Vanguard banners, oil-lamp orange window light, worked earth tones. The town reads as busy-but-orderly — soldiers and civilians mixing in planned pedestrian flow. Son's era additions: expanded timber palisade tiles on three sides of the Garrison Square, refugee tent clusters (patched canvas, guy-ropes, small cook-fires) along the southern edge, a graffito-marked lapsed-banner tile on the old notice board, boarded-up civilian storefront tiles, trampled-mud tiles replacing the original paving near the canyon mouth. The old cooperative red-and-steel banners are replaced in son's era with a colder gray-on-gray Vanguard sigil.

### ambience

Father's era: a military-working-town soundscape. Marching boots on flagstones in staggered patterns (a squad drilling distantly), the rhythmic ring of a farrier's hammer, horses snorting in the stables, a sergeant calling cadence, cartwheels over cobblestone, wood being stacked, the occasional laugh or shouted greeting between civilians and off-duty soldiers. Underneath: wind funneled down out of the canyon, a constant low flow that picks up at the northern edge of town. Son's era: the drill cadence is still there but fewer voices, and the civilians are absent from the central streets — replaced by the muted noise of the refugee camp at the southern edge (children, cooking, low conversations, the crack of canvas in the wind). The farrier's hammer still rings but the pattern is different — armoring work rather than horseshoeing. The canyon wind is unchanged. It was always loudest here. It is still loudest here. Add, in the son's era, the distant clang of the checkpoint gate opening and closing at unpredictable intervals — a sound the refugees flinch at.

### music

Father's era: a march in E minor led by snare drum and low brass. Not menacing — professional. The snare cadence is crisp at a parade-step tempo (120 bpm), with a tenor horn carrying a straightforward melodic line that suggests duty done competently. A bass drum anchors on the one and three. The piece has a hummable middle passage on a solo fife that brings the military scaffolding down to human scale — the sound of a soldier off duty, whistling on his way home. Son's era: the same march cadence but stripped of the fife, and the brass line is transposed a minor third down, making it heavier and more oppressive. The snare drum now plays slightly behind the beat, a drag that suggests institutional fatigue. Beneath the march, a solo fiddle plays a folk melody from the refugee camp — a different song, in a different key, that never syncs with the march above it. The two musics coexist in the same space without agreeing.
