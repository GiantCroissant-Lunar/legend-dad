# Saltmere Port

## Overview

A working fishing and trading port on the eastern coast, half a day's travel southeast of Thornwall along the old coast road. Saltmere is built where a chalk river empties into the Moonlit Sea, its two stone breakwaters curving out to shelter a deep-water harbor. In Kaelen's era it is the busiest inland-coast port in the region — fishing crews come in on the morning tide, coastal traders unload cloth and salt, and the harbormaster's office logs a ship a day. In Aric's era the harbor is silting up: mineral sediment washes down from the corrupted uplands and settles in the shallows, the catch has thinned, and half the docks have rotted. What remains operates on private arrangements rather than guild charter.

## Atmosphere & Appearance

Saltmere smells of fish, tar, and wet rope. Timber warehouses stand shoulder-to-shoulder along the waterfront, their sides weathered silver-gray from salt spray. Above the warehouses rise the older stone buildings of the merchant quarter — three-story townhouses with iron balconies overlooking the harbor. The streets are paved with worn flagstones, uneven where the salt has lifted the mortar. In Kaelen's era, fishing boats and coastal traders crowd the harbor, ropes whipping in the sea wind, and the harbormaster's tower flies the port's blue-and-white pennant. In Aric's era, the same harbor holds perhaps a third as many vessels, many of them half-sunk or stripped for parts. A pale film coats the shallows where the river meets the sea, and the water's edge is crusted with a thin scum of mineral residue that crunches underfoot like frost.

## History

Saltmere began as a seasonal fishing camp two centuries ago, before the Thornbriar settlements existed. Its permanent population grew when inland traders started routing their coastal shipments through the chalk-river mouth rather than the more dangerous cliffs further north. The port's guild charter was signed eighty years ago, establishing Saltmere as a self-governing trade town under the protection — but not the jurisdiction — of the lowland militias. The Vanguard established a quartering right here a generation ago, never much enforced, but in the son's era the Vanguard has begun pressing that right to control what moves through the harbor. Three years before Aric's quest begins, the chalk river started running cloudy and the shallower fishing grounds collapsed. The guild has not officially acknowledged this yet. Everyone knows.

## Notable Features

- The Twin Breakwaters — two curving stone moles extending a quarter-mile into the Moonlit Sea, lined with mooring bollards. In father's era: a beacon fire burns on the seaward end of each mole. In son's era: only the landward beacon is still lit.
- The Harbormaster's Tower — a four-story stone tower at the base of the north breakwater, flying the port pennant. Cael Vire's office is on the third floor. Contains two decades of harbor logs that Aric can consult.
- The Low Docks — the older, wooden docks on the southern side of the harbor, used mainly by fishing crews. Cheaper mooring, less supervision. In son's era: half rotted out, the remaining slips used by smaller traders who prefer not to be logged.
- The Saltscale Inn — the port's main inn, owned by Brine, on the waterfront between the Low Docks and the merchant quarter. Large common room, rooms above the kitchen, a private back table reserved for harbormaster business.
- The Chalk Mouth — where the river meets the sea. A wide, shallow estuary with reed beds and a derelict tide mill. In father's era: the tide mill is operational and grinds salt. In son's era: the mill is abandoned and the estuary water runs silver-gray with suspended sediment.
- Nella's Fish Stall — a permanent wooden stall at the foot of the Low Docks, where Nella has sold dockside fish for thirty years. Unofficial gossip center of the town.

## Creative Prompts

### environment-art

16-bit pixel art tileset, top-down perspective. Coastal town tiles: gray-silver weathered timber warehouse walls with rust-stained metal braces, worn flagstone streets with patches of wet stone, sloping wooden dock tiles with rope bollard props, stone breakwater tiles curving at the edges. Harbor water tiles: a clear teal-blue for deep water in the father's era, graduated to a muddy gray-green in the shallows, with foam-edge tiles at the dock lines. Fishing boat sprites: small single-masted dinghies, larger two-masted coastal traders, nets draped over gunwales. Father's era palette: saturated sea blues, warm tan wood, white-painted mooring posts, colorful pennants (red, yellow, blue). Son's era additions: silted shallow-water tiles with a pale mineral film, rotted dock tiles with gap-toothed planks, abandoned boat hulls leaning against pilings, scum-crusted waterline tiles. The chalk river estuary gets special tiles: in father's era clear turquoise water with green reed beds, in son's era a silver-gray silted outflow with dead reed stubble. Harbormaster's tower is a tall stone landmark visible from anywhere in the port.

### ambience

Father's era: a layered working-port soundscape. Gulls calling overhead in overlapping choruses, waves slapping against pilings at slow intervals, rope creaking against bollards, ship bells at varied pitches and distances, the rhythmic thunk of a caulker's hammer, a fishmonger calling prices, rope reels clacking, hulls bumping dock rubrails. Undergirding it all is the constant low rush of surf on the breakwaters. Son's era: the same base soundscape but thinned and distorted. Fewer gulls, spaced farther apart. The caulker's hammer is absent. The fishmonger's call is heard only once, not repeated. The breakwater surf has a new quality — a faint hiss where the sediment in the water froths differently against the stones. Add a new low sound: the slow grinding shift of silted mud in the shallows, audible only when the tide pulls back.

### music

Father's era: a sea-shanty-inflected piece in D major, led by a concertina (accordion family) playing a lilting melody at a moderate walking tempo. Hand drums and a tambourine provide a rolling, oceanic rhythm. A tin whistle adds ornamental trills over the main melody like birds over the harbor. The piece has a verse-chorus feel — expect villagers to hum it. Son's era: the same concertina melody, but slower, fewer ornaments, and the hand drum is replaced by a single bass drum struck softly at long intervals like a distant bell buoy. The tin whistle is gone. A solo fiddle enters in the second half of the loop with a counter-melody in the relative minor, adding a note of stubborn endurance — the port has not given up, even if the sea has changed.
