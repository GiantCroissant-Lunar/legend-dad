# Warden Jessa Vale

## Overview

Warden of Lastwatch, the stone keep between the Iron Peaks and Starlight Academy. Jessa is a tall, spare woman with prematurely gray hair that she keeps cropped short above her ears, sharp cheekbones, and eyes the pale gray of a winter sky. She took the warden's post at twenty-two and has not left the keep since. In Kaelen's era, she is in her early thirties and has been warden for ten years — a respected figure among the joint-militia council that commissioned the keep, with a reputation for clean signal discipline and clear-eyed reporting. In Aric's era, she is fifty-two and has been warden for thirty years; she is the only living soul in a keep that has been partially consumed by crystal growth from the rock below, and no one from any faction has visited her in six years. She has kept the signal fire lit the whole time.

## Backstory

Jessa was born in a coastal militia town and named for her grandmother Hesper Vale, the warden who gave Lastwatch its last famous wrong signal fifty years ago. That signal — sent one winter night during a rockslide — reported an enemy raid that was not happening, and by the time the militias arrived at the keep, three of Hesper's own had died trying to correct the signal before reinforcements wasted a night's march. Hesper was not formally punished; the joint-militia council ruled the signal "an error of observation in poor visibility" and carved her name into the courtyard stone. The stone, and the shame of it, shaped Jessa's childhood. She enlisted in the coastal militia at sixteen with the explicit intention of earning a Lastwatch posting, trained in signal discipline and mountain observation, and received her warden's commission at twenty-two — the youngest warden in the keep's history. Six years before Aric's quest, when the joint-militia council dissolved under the Vanguard's new doctrine and the other factions ceased supporting the keep, Jessa was ordered to abandon her post. She acknowledged the order, thanked the council, and did not leave. No one has come to remove her.

## Personality & Motivation

Jessa is precise, patient, and not unkind. She speaks sparingly, but she answers direct questions directly. She has the composure of someone who has lived alone with her own thoughts long enough to have finished most of the arguments. She will offer a visitor tea from her tower hearth without comment on who they are or why they have come; the offer is simply what one does when the wind is cold. Her primary motivation is keeping the signal fire lit, the logs current, and the keep's one remaining function intact — Lastwatch was built to watch the passes, and she watches them. Her deeper motivation, which she has never spoken aloud to anyone, is that she believes, without evidence she can articulate, that there will come a night when the signal will matter again and that her grandmother's name will be answered for. She has not been right about this so far. She is also not going to be wrong first.

## Relationships

- [[Lastwatch]] — her post for thirty years; she refers to it as "the keep" and never as "home," though it has been both
- [[Iron Peaks]] — the mountains she watches from the signal platform; she knows their rockfall patterns better than any living person
- [[Starlight Academy]] — one of the three factions that commissioned the keep; she once sent a signal that saved a research expedition, and the Academy used to send her a copy of every major star-chart they produced as courtesy — the last one arrived six years ago
- [[Kaelen]] — in the father's era, he passes through the keep during his Iron Peaks mission; she gives him tea, exchanges twelve sentences with him, and signals his departure to Ashenford and the Academy per protocol
- [[Aric]] — in the son's era, he finds her still at her post, and she recognizes his father's line before he introduces himself

## Creative Prompts

### portrait

16-bit pixel art, grayscale base with palette-swap tinting. A tall, spare woman with short-cropped gray hair and sharp cheekbones. Pale gray eyes, steady gaze. In the father's era: mid-thirties, wears a warden's wool coat in the dark blue of the joint-militia (older, cooperative sigil on the collar — a triangle of three flames for the three commissioning factions), a leather belt with a signaling-horn and a small iron pulley key. Her hands are strong and lined from rope work. In the son's era: early fifties, the same coat now patched at the elbows and lighter gray at the shoulders from thirty years of sun through the tower window, the cooperative sigil still on the collar though the council that wore it has dissolved. Her hands are the same. Cold silver-blue lighting from a high window in both versions; Jessa is lit as if the signal fire above were always just out of frame.

### voice

Clear mezzo-soprano with a precise, unhurried cadence. She does not fill silences. Her sentences are complete and short. When she tells a visitor what to do, she does so as a statement of fact ("The tea is to your left. The wind will drop at dusk."), not as a command. She has a small, private laugh — more a breath than a sound — that she uses when someone says something accidentally true. No regional accent; thirty years of solitary logs have flattened it. When she speaks of her grandmother, her voice goes slower rather than softer, as if she were reading aloud from an older book.

### theme-music

Solo violin in A minor, played high on the instrument's register, with a clean unwavering tone. The melody is a long line that rises gradually across sixteen bars and descends in a shorter phrase, then repeats — the shape of a warden's scan across the horizon and back. A low sustained drone on a bowed psaltery sits beneath, unchanging. In the father's era, a second violin enters quietly in the second half and plays in thirds with the first — cooperative counterpoint, a reminder that the keep was made by multiple hands. In the son's era, the second violin is gone. The first violin plays the same line, alone, at the same unwavering tone. What has been lost is not in the melody. It is in everything the melody used to share the air with.
