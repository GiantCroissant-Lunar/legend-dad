# Captain Rhen Halloway

## Overview

Captain of the Ashenford garrison in the father's era, one of the last Iron Vanguard officers who served under the old cooperative doctrine before the militarization. Rhen is tall, lean, and precisely groomed in his Vanguard grays, with short iron-gray hair and a small scar at the corner of his mouth from an arrow graze he took escorting a salt caravan as a junior officer. In Kaelen's era he is forty-five, fifteen years into his Ashenford command, and widely respected by both his soldiers and the lowland villages that depend on the mountain road he keeps open. In Aric's era, Rhen is sixty-five and has been retired for six years; he still lives in Ashenford, in a small stone house at the edge of the refugee camp, and he has not worn a uniform since the day he turned in his commission in protest of the new doctrine.

## Backstory

Rhen enlisted in the Vanguard at seventeen, coming out of a tenant-farmer family from the coastal plain. He rose through the ranks as a patrol officer, built a reputation for clean judgment and for refusing bribes from merchants who wanted to move unlisted cargo through the Peaks, and earned his captaincy at thirty. The Ashenford command came to him at thirty because the previous captain had died of a stroke and the Vanguard high command wanted someone who would keep the lowland councils comfortable — Rhen was that man. For the next fifteen years, he ran Ashenford as a cooperative garrison, attending village council meetings as often as parade drills, and kept the road to the Peaks open with a patrol system that was efficient without being oppressive. Six years before Aric's quest, after the deep-tunnel resonance incident that killed a patrol squad and changed the doctrine, Rhen was ordered to begin fortifying the canyon mouth into a permanent checkpoint and to cease cooperative agreements with the lowland councils. He refused. He was offered a transfer to a coastal posting. He refused that too. He turned in his commission on a cold morning in early spring and walked out of Ashenford's Garrison Square in civilian clothes, and he has not been back inside it since.

## Personality & Motivation

In Kaelen's era, Rhen is calm, formal, and quietly warm — a professional man with a strong sense of obligation to both the institution he serves and the communities it was meant to serve. He speaks plainly and listens carefully. He has a subtle sense of humor that surfaces over shared meals and disappears during duty hours. His primary motivation is keeping the mountain road open for everyone who needs it — militia, miners, merchants, foresters — and he believes, with real conviction, that the Vanguard at its best is a service to the lowlands rather than a force over them. In Aric's era, Rhen is a broken and stubborn old man. He has not forgiven himself for not seeing the militarization coming, for the patrol squad that died underground, or for the lowland communities his refusal could not save. His motivation is smaller now: he helps, one person at a time, when people come to him. He does not lead anyone. He will not.

## Creative Prompts

### portrait

16-bit pixel art, grayscale base with palette-swap tinting. A tall, lean man with short iron-gray hair, clean-shaven, a small pale scar at the corner of his mouth. In the father's era: Vanguard dress grays — a wool coat with black iron buttons and a steel-and-red cooperative shoulder insignia (the old doctrine's sigil), a polished leather belt, a short sword at his hip. His posture is straight without being rigid. His eyes are steady, slightly tired. In the son's era: the same man at sixty-five, wearing a plain civilian coat of undyed wool over a linen shirt, no belt, no sword, no insignia. The hair is fully silver. The posture is slightly stooped at the shoulders — not from age, from something heavier. The eyes are the same. Cold gray overcast lighting in both versions; Rhen does not look right in warm light.

### voice

Clear baritone with precise diction and a faint coastal-plain cadence that he has never fully trained out. Speaks in complete sentences, often with a brief pause before the final clause while he chooses between two words. Has a distinctive habit of repeating a question back before answering it ("Why the road. Because the road is the town's blood. Anything else?"). In the father's era, his voice carries institutional authority without shouting; he never raises his voice because he does not need to. In the son's era, his voice is softer and slower — not weaker, just quieter. He still pauses before the final clause. He still chooses between two words.

### theme-music

Solo trumpet in D minor, muted (with a Harmon mute or equivalent), played at the tempo of a slow formal march. The melodic line is dignified and slightly melancholy, built from short phrases separated by deliberate rests — the musical equivalent of Rhen's speech pattern. A low string drone provides the ground. In the father's era, a snare drum enters in the second half of the piece, unmuted, playing a slow cadence underneath the trumpet — the institution still present behind the man. In the son's era, the snare drum is gone entirely; the trumpet plays alone, and the drone beneath it has thinned to a single sustained note. The piece is the same. What has been taken away is everything around it.
